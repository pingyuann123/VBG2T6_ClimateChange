Introduction

Urbanization is often known as the movement of people from rural areas to cities, driving economic and infrastructural development growth.

As a country becomes more urbanized, its impact on climate change intensifies, particularly in terms of worsening air quality, increasing Urban Heat Island (UHI) effects, and rising temperatures

Problem

While urban countries have reaped many benefits from urbanization, from improved infrastructure, technological advancements, and economic prosperity, they also contribute disproportionately to climate change due to higher energy consumption, industrial activity, and emissions from transportation.

Urbanization impact on climate change had intensified, p articularly in terms of worsening air quality, increasing Urban Heat Island (UHI) effects, and rising temperatures. As such, addressing their climate impact through strategic policies, sustainable urban planning, and green infrastructure becomes increasingly urgent.

Motivation

According to NASA, global surface temperatures have risen by 1.2°C since the late 19th century, with urban areas experiencing even greater temperature increases due to the Urban Heat Island (UHI) effect. Major economic hubs in highly-urbanised countries have seen temperature rises of 3–5°C in recent decades.

Additionally, air quality deterioration, driven by industrial activity, vehicular emissions, and energy consumption, is another critical issue tied to urbanization. These environmental stressors not only impact daily life but also , contributing to long-term climate instability, making effective urban planning more critical than ever.

Given that, we hope to identify key contributing factors, examine the relationship between urbanisation, air quality, and local climate shifts. By identifying trends in temperature changes, pollution levels, and mitigation strategies, we seek to provide valuable insights for sustainable urban development. Understanding these patterns will enable policymakers, urban planners, and communities to implement strategies that balance growth with environmental responsibility, ensuring a healthier and more sustainable future

Urbanization

What is Urbanisation?

Urbanisation is commonly understood as the movement of people from rural areas to cities, often associated with rising population density and economic opportunity. But in reality, urbanisation looks different across countries. Some define it by city population, others by industrial activity or even infrastructure access.

In our analysis, we explore how urbanisation is shaped not just by where people live, but by how economies evolve — through GDP growth, industrial development, and shifts in rural-urban demographics. The visualisations below aim to uncover how these factors interplay to shape what it means for a country to be urban.

Climate Change Impacts

Urbanization has undeniably driven industrialization, leading to significant environmental shifts. As cities expand, we see a direct impact on three critical variables — rising temperatures, declining air quality, and increasing CO₂ emissions — all of which contribute to climate change.


This impact analysis examines both the positive and negative consequences of urbanization, exploring how unchecked industrial expansion can worsen climate-related disasters and public health challenges. Through data-driven insights, we aim to highlight the urgent need for sustainable urban planning and mitigation efforts.
