Introduction

Urbanization is often known as the movement of people from rural areas to cities, driving economic and infrastructural development growth.

As a country becomes more urbanized, its impact on climate change intensifies, particularly in terms of worsening air quality, increasing Urban Heat Island (UHI) effects, and rising temperatures

Problem

As a country becomes more urbanized, its impact on climate change intensifies, particularly in terms of worsening air quality, increasing Urban Heat Island (UHI) effects, and rising temperatures. As such, addressing their climate impact through strategic policies, sustainable urban planning, and green infrastructure becomes increasingly urgent.

Motivation

According to NASA, global surface temperatures have risen by 1.2°C since the late 19th century, with urban areas experiencing even greater temperature increases due to the Urban Heat Island (UHI) effect. Major economic hubs in highly-urbanised countries, such as Beijing and New York, have seen temperature rises of 3–5°C in recent decades due to dense infrastructure and limited green spaces. In Delhi and Jakarta, PM2.5 concentrations have surged beyond 70 µg/m³, far exceeding the WHO-recommended level of 5 µg/m³ (IQAir, 2023). This long-term exposure to poor air quality is linked to increased cases of respiratory diseases, leading to reduced life expectancy. (American Lung Association, 2020)

Additionally, air quality deterioration, driven by industrial activity, vehicular emissions, and energy consumption, is another critical issue tied to urbanization. Cities like Delhi and Jakarta have reported PM2.5 concentrations exceeding 70 µg/m³, far above the WHO-recommended level of 5 µg/m³ (IQAir, 2023).

The effects of urbanisation on climate are evident in the increasing prevalence of urban heat islands (UHI), where densely built environments contribute to significant temperature rises. Simultaneously, air pollution, driven by industrial activity, vehicular emissions, and energy consumption, has led to declining air quality, posing serious health risks to urban populations. These environmental stressors not only impact daily life but also , contributing to long-term climate instability, making effective urban planning more critical than ever.

Datasets

  • Global Urbanization Trends (United Nations, n.d.)
    • This dataset provides insights into urban and rural population distributions, enabling analysis of rapid urbanization’s links to economic, environmental, and social factors. Urbanization Rate vs GDP (Our World in Data, n.d.)
  • Ambient Air Quality Database (WHO, 2024)
    • This dataset offers a global overview of air quality, tracking annual mean PM2.5 and PM10 concentrations to analyze trends and urbanization’s impact on pollution. Global Surface Temperature Data (Global Data Lab, n.d.)
  • Global Surface Temperature Data (Global Data Lab, n.d.)
    • This dataset records annual mean surface temperatures across geographic levels, allowing comparisons to determine whether urbanized areas experience greater warming. CO₂ and Greenhouse Gas Emissions (GHG) Dataset (Our World in Data, 2024)
  • CO₂ and Greenhouse Gas Emissions (GHG) Dataset (Our World in Data, 2024)
    • This dataset provides global, national, and sector-specific CO₂ and GHG emissions, enabling cross-country comparisons, and analysis of urbanization’s impact on climate change and policy effectiveness.
