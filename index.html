Temperature & Urban Heat Index (UHI)

This section aims to analyze the impact of urbanization on surface temperatures and the intensity of the Urban Heat Index (UHI) effect.


Temperature:
Temperature refers to the measure of heat in the atmosphere, which varies across regions due to factors such as geography, season, and urbanization. Urbanization has been shown to exacerbate global warming, especially in cities, due to the Urban Heat Index (UHI) effect. In urban areas, temperatures tend to be higher than in surrounding rural areas, primarily due to dense construction, reduced green spaces, and increased energy consumption.

Urban Heat Index (UHI):
The UHI effect refers to the localized temperature difference between urban and rural areas, where urban environments typically experience higher temperatures. This phenomenon is driven by human activities, dense infrastructure, and heat absorption by materials such as concrete and asphalt. The UHI effect not only intensifies the broader impacts of climate change but also poses significant health risks. Prolonged exposure to elevated temperatures in urban areas can increase the likelihood of heat-related illnesses, particularly among vulnerable populations.

Through this analysis, we aim to understand how urban growth contributes to temperature increases and exacerbates climate change.
